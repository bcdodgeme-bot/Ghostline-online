Summary

Tim has always been good at asking the right questions, and this short PDF packs a lot of punch.

These are a staple of my daily journaling practice.

Questions

The 17 questions:

  1. What if I did the opposite for 48 hours?
  2. What do I spend a silly amount of money on? How might I scratch my own itch?
  3. What would I do/have/be if I had $10 million? What's my real TMI?
  4. What are the worst things that could happen? Could I get back here?
  5. If I could only work 2 hours per week on my business, what would I do?
  6. "What 20% of the customers/products/regions are producing 80% of the profit?"
  7. "What factors or shared characteristics might account for this?"
  8. What if I let them make decisions up to $100? $500? $1000?
  9. What's the least crowded channel?
  10. What if I couldn't pitch my product directly?
  11. What if I created my own real-world MBA?
  12. Do I need to make it back the way I lost it?
  13. What if I could only subtract to solve problems?
  14. "What should I put on my not-to-do list?"
  15. What might I put in place to allow me to go off the grid for 4 to 8 weeks, with no phone or email?
  16. Am I hunting antelope or field mice?
  17. Which one of these, if done, would render all the rest either easier or completely irrelevant?
  18. Could it be that everything is fine and complete as it is?
  19. What would this look like if it were easy?
  20. How can I throw money at this problem? How can I "waste" money to improve the quality of my life?
  21. No hurry, no pause

Detailed Notes

Reality is largely negotiable.

If you stress-test the boundaries and experiment with the "impossibles," you'll quickly discover that most limitations are a fragile collection of socially reinforced rules you can choose to break at any time.

If I could only work 2 hours per week on my business, what would I do?

After reading The E-Myth Revisited by Michael Gerber and The 80/20 Principle by Riichard Koch, I decided that extreme questions were the forcing function I needed.

"What 20% of the customers/products/regions are producing 80% of the profit?"

"What factors or shared characteristics might account for this?"

What if I let them make decisions up to $100? $500? $1000?

This question allowed me to take my customer service workload from 40-to-60 hours per week to less than 2 hours per week.

This experience underscored two things for me:

  1. To get huge, good things done, you need to be okay with letting the small, bad things happen, and
  2. People's IQs seem to double as soon as you give them responsibility and indicate you trust them.
