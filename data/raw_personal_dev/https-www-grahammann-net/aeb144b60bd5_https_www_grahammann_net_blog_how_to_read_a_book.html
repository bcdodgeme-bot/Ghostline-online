Syntopical Reading

The answer to this question is syntopical reading.

Syntopical reading: reading many books on the same subject, then comparing and contrasting the ideas within.

The benefit of this method is that you get much more depth.

Ideas and arguments are fresh in your mind. After reading several books, you can examine your notes and come up with general principles and ideas.

So, with this in mind, how do you choose what to read?

The second step is to schedule this self-testing appropriately, and set aside time for it.

The general rule for retaining material is to test yourself at 10-20% of the interval you want to retain it. Â For example, if you want to remember something for a year, you should revisit the material at least every month or two.

How to Read a Book Summary

To get the most from reading:

  1. Choose a subject youâd like to learn.
  2. Pick the best 3-5 books on the topic, from different authors.
  3. Use recommendations from experts, not general reviews or lists.
  4. Write down the question(s) you want answered.
  5. Take notes:
  6. Highlight key passages and write notes while reading.
  7. Briefly summarize each chapter in your own words.
  8. Write down new questions and ways you could immediately apply the knowledge to your own life.
  9. Revisit all notes after a week or so, and rewrite and format to make them useful for you.
  10. Store somewhere easy to access (I use Evernote).
  11. Use the Feynman technique to synthesize information.
  12. Choose concept (the question you set out to answer).
  13. Teach to a toddler.
  14. Identify gaps & go back to source material.
  15. Review & simplify.
  16. Write down how you could immediately apply new knowledge.
  17. To retain information long-term:
  18. Use self-testing.
  19. Revisit every 1-2 months.
  20. As you read more books on the topic, update notes and repeat.
